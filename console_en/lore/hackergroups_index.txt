TITLE: Hacker Groups and Survival Networks

What follows is not a neutral directory.
It is a partial map of the structures that still hold when the official city decides certain neighborhoods can wait, stay silent, or disappear.
People call them hacker groups, but the term is too narrow. Most of these networks do far more than breach systems: they repair lines, move evidence, train beginners, hide people, rebuild services, and absorb the human damage no dashboard wants to name.

GhostMesh:
GhostMesh is not one central organization but a constellation of decentralized relays, careful operators, and protocols passed from mouth to mouth and terminal to terminal. The network favors low-signature operations, clean transfers, minimal traces, and goals whose value exceeds any need for recognition.
Strength: discipline under pressure, strong operational hygiene, and the ability to keep channels alive where everything looks dead.
Weakness: trust takes a long time to build, and paranoia sometimes slows response until the useful window is already closing.

Roofline Collective (ZE08):
Roofline is made up of technicians, rogue electricians, maintenance improvisers, and neighbors who are simply too competent to let a building die alone. Their specialty is not spectacle. It is the concrete thing: reconnect a pump, harden a repeater, repair a door, patch a power feed, protect a rooftop so the district keeps signal and a little dignity.
Strength: local legitimacy, memory of failures, real human ties to residents, and the ability to turn technical skill into visible mutual aid.
Weakness: exposed infrastructure, chronic exhaustion, and too little redundancy when too few hands know how to repair too many things.

Free Kernel Assembly (DX90 lineage):
The Free Kernel Assembly is what happens when open-source radicals refuse to let code become only a commodity or a weapon of social management. The DX90 lineage maintains covert toolchains, mirrors, archives, and software commons that survive seizure waves and repository purges.
Strength: intellectual and technical resilience, extensive documentation when it exists, and the ability to rebuild tools from almost nothing.
Weakness: endless internal debates, ideological fractures, and wildly uneven operational readiness across cells. Some are brilliant. Others still confuse doctrinal purity with practical usefulness.

Night Couriers:
When the network becomes too watched, too slow, or too poisoned to move a packet, the Night Couriers return to an old truth: sometimes the best bandwidth is still a body in motion. They physically carry files, modules, keys, medical datasets, and sometimes witnesses no system should ever see crossing a boundary.
Strength: difficult to trace at scale, tactically flexible, and able to bypass entire infrastructures by refusing to use them.
Weakness: high personal risk, almost no legal cover, and irreplaceable losses when a courier falls, because you do not easily replace someone who knows the routes, the faces, and the useful silences.

General note:
None of these groups are pure.
They argue, make mistakes, burn out, keep too many secrets, or react too slowly to weak signals.
But without them, the megastructures would already have turned most districts into low-priority service zones.
What official order calls pockets of instability is often, from the ground, the last place where anyone is still trying to make something work for real.
